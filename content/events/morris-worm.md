The Morris Worm, created by Robert Tappan Morris, was the first computer worm distributed via the Internet. On November 2, 1988, this self-replicating program exploited vulnerabilities in Unix systems, including a buffer overflow in fingerd and weak passwords.

## Impact

The worm infected an estimated 6,000 computers, which was approximately 10% of the Internet at the time. It caused significant disruption by consuming system resources and making infected machines unusable.

## Legal Consequences

Robert Morris became the first person convicted under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986. He was sentenced to three years of probation, 400 hours of community service, and a fine of $10,050.

## Legacy

This incident led to:
- Formation of the Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT)
- Increased awareness of Internet security
- Development of better security practices and tools
- Recognition that security vulnerabilities could have widespread impact

The Morris Worm demonstrated that a single security flaw could have cascading effects across the entire network, a lesson that remains relevant in modern application security.
